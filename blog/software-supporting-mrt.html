The software supporting single-dish radiotelescopes

Introduction

A telescope is a system of systems with the purpose of collecting radiation from a desired celestial body. As all systems, the telescope's ability to fulfill its purpose is hindered by the environment in which it operates. Consequently, the efficiency with which the telescope fulfills its purpose, is a product of its sub-systems' efficiencies. An efficient telescope is therefore one which can operate efficiently no matter its environmental conditions. The difficulties in operating a telescope efficiently branches, firstly, from the natural requirements of collecting radiation, and secondly, from what it physically entails to fulfill those requirements. Following this reasoning, one can conceptually build a tree of requirements which reflects the importance and the physicality of each requirement in fulfilling the system's overall purpose. A millimeter radiotelescope, hereafter MRT, is a telescope which collects millimeter radiation, and is thus said to "operate in the radio regime". The natural requirements of a ground-based MRT are, firstly, to operate at an altitude above most of the atmospheric water-vapor layers, secondly, to have an optically-adequate surface radius and thickness to reflect and focus millimeter radiation, and thirdly, to point and track sources with high precision and be able to do so for extended periods of time to detect statistically significant signals.
